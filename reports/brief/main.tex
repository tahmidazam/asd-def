\section*{Background}

Litman et al.\ \cite{litmanDecompositionPhenotypicHeterogeneity2025a} fit a mixture model to
parent-reported phenotype data from SPARK and recovered 4 autism classes (Social or behavioral,
Mixed ASD with developmental delay, Moderate challenges, and Broadly affected), which they map to
genetic programs of common, de novo, and inherited variation and present as biologically
grounded. Here, we test whether these classes are invariant to diagnostic era and age at
diagnosis.

\section*{The gap}

Litman et al.\ enter age at evaluation as a model covariate and use age at diagnosis only for
external validation. No peer-reviewed work has tested whether their classes are confounded by
age at diagnosis or diagnostic timing. Recent literature has questioned the stability of the autism diagnosis through time: age at diagnosis has been shown to index different
developmental and polygenic profiles~\cite{zhangPolygenicDevelopmentalProfiles2025}; and the
mean polygenic load of an ASD diagnosis declines with diagnostic year, with broadening criteria
serving as a candidate explanation~\cite{labiancaChangesGeneticContributions2026}. These
investigations discuss autism at the population level; whether Litman's classes agree with, or
are confounded by, these same population-level shifts is untested.

\section*{Why it matters}

A data-driven autism class becomes consequential the moment people are sorted into it, for
research, for clinical narratives, and eventually for care. Who is diagnosed early, and who is
diagnosed at all, has never been random: it tracks sex, race, and access to services. A partition that is not invariant to diagnostic era and age at diagnosis risks mistaking the history of who was noticed, and when, for structure intrinsic to the disorder. Testing that invariance is what this work is for.

$H_0^{A}$: The four-class phenotypic profiles are invariant to diagnostic era/age at
diagnosis. &
Hold the four classes as Litman defined them and slide a window along each axis, recomputing each
class's centroid. The distance a centroid
travels, expressed in separation units (mean inter-class distance), is the drift
(a systematic shift of a class's profile along the axis; \autoref{fig:traj}). Resampling whole
families (a bootstrap, giving an uncertainty band) shows whether the shift is real, and we confirm
it is larger than for a random re-ordering, which should produce none (\autoref{fig:atlas}). &
\rej, $p<0.001$ (drift, both axes; mean drift beats the random baseline at the bootstrap floor).
All four classes clear the random control on both axes, the era margins slimmer than age. \\
\midrule

$H_0^{B}$: The class proportions are invariant to diagnostic era/age at diagnosis. &
Model each person's class as a function of the axis (multinomial logistic regression: the odds
of each class as the axis moves). The three-step method carries through the fact that class
membership is estimated rather than observed, so a person's uncertainty about which class they
belong to does not bias the trend. Uncertainty from the same family-resampling bootstrap. &
\rej, $p<0.01$ (all four classes, both axes, per-class; bootstrap floor; joint
likelihood-ratio test saturates). \\
\midrule

$H_0^{C}$: The optimal number of clusters is four and invariant to diagnostic era/age at
diagnosis. &
Refit the mixture within coarse slices of the axis and choose the number of classes by which
fits held-out data best (cross-validated log-likelihood), with guards against unstable
solutions, plus a formal \ac{em} test of different $k$ values. &
\sopen{Not yet run.} \\
\midrule

$H_0^{D}$: The drift is within sampling noise and small relative to the between-class
separation. [requires $H_0^{A}$ rejected] &
Read the same centroid movement as an absolute size relative to the between-class gap, with its
bootstrap band (\autoref{fig:atlas}). A capture fraction (the share of the movement lying in the
plane that separates the classes) flags drift that points somewhere else, off the axes that define
the classes. &
\rej, $p<0.001$ (magnitude exceeds sampling noise; distinct from A's specificity
floor). \\
\midrule

$H_0^{E}$: The drift is inconsistent in direction. [requires $H_0^{A}$ rejected] &
For each class, measure the slope of its trajectory (does it move steadily one way along the
axis?), with a two-sided family-resampling bootstrap and a correction for testing four classes
across two axes (Benjamini-Hochberg, controlling the false-discovery rate). &
\rej{} for 7 of 8 class-by-axis tests, $q<0.05$. \\
\midrule

$H_0^{F}$: The drift is spread evenly across the seven phenotypic categories.
[requires $H_0^{A}$ rejected] &
Break the drift down into the four classes by the seven phenotype categories, with a confidence
interval per feature and false-discovery-rate control. Because this splits one quantity into
parts, it yields a per-feature count rather than a single $p$-value. &
\rej{} (concentrated, not uniform: Mixed-ASD-DD on developmental (43\%/64\% era/age),
the rest social-communication-led on era; \autoref{fig:cats}. \\
\midrule

$H_0^{G}$: The drift is spread evenly across instruments regardless of referent (current vs
retrospective). [requires $H_0^{A}$ rejected] &
Split the era drift by what each questionnaire asks about: current state versus retrospective
(lifetime) history. Compare the two per class with a size-fair contrast (root-mean-square, so the larger
current-state group does not win on feature count) and a paired family-resampling bootstrap. &
\rej, $p<0.01$ (all four classes retrospective-dominant; BH across the four): the
era drift tracks lifetime-referent report, a shifting diagnosed population. \\
\midrule

$H_0^{H}$: The drift is spread evenly across measurement lag (age at diagnosis vs age at
evaluation). [requires $H_0^{A}$ rejected] &
Separate the effect of diagnostic year from the lag between age at diagnosis and age at
evaluation, using a direct-effect model (differential item functioning: does year shift an
answer once the person's class and evaluation age are held fixed?), with a small-lag
sensitivity re-test. &
\sopen{Partially written.} \\
\midrule

$H_0^{I}$: The genotype-to-class mapping is invariant to diagnostic era/age at diagnosis.
[gated on data, not on a rejection] &
Test whether the axis changes how genotype predicts class: an interaction between class and the
axis (Wald or likelihood-ratio test) in a model over polygenic scores and rare-variant burden,
adjusting for cognitive impairment. Class labels stay fixed, so no mixture is refitted. &
\sopen{Waiting on data access.} \\
\midrule

$H_0^{J}$: Any drift in the genotype-to-class mapping tracks the drift in the phenotype.
[requires $H_0^{A}$ rejected] &
Cross-tabulate the two outcomes per axis: where the profile drifted ($H_0^{A}$) against where
the genotype-to-class map drifted ($H_0^{I}$). &
\sopen{Waiting on data access.} \\
